Segregation in Cook County, Illinois

Despite landmark legislation passed in the 1960s, including the Civil Rights Act, Voting Rights Act, and Fair Housing Act, Cook County remains divided. By scrolling through this page, you can see how segregation in America has evolved since 1970.

The human costs of such stagnation are tremendous. Despite the steps forward America has made regarding segregation, Cook County is coming apart in other ways. Worsening inequality in education, income, and high school test scores seems to be driving a dispiriting trend.

For those concerned about the American dream, this trend is disturbing.

What is the Dissimilarity Index?

A common measure of segregation used by social scientists is the dissimilarity index (DI), which ranges from 100, reflecting a complete clustering of a group in a separate neighborhood, to 0, indicating a perfectly even distribution. Ultimately, the DI calculates the share of a given race/ethnicity that would have to move to produce a fully integrated Cook County.

Cook County's Dissimilarity Index (DI)



How Segregated Are Racial/Ethnic Groups in Cook County?

In 1970, the dissimilarity index was 93% among Blacks and Whites in Cook County. While the dissimilarity index has declined since then, it still stands at 75% -- meaning that 75% of Blacks and White would have to move to produce a fully integrated county.

When it comes to residential integration, northern Democratic strongholds like Cook County seem stuck in the past. Not only is it one of America’s most segregated counties, but it has also shown meager improvement. The persistence of unmixed Black and Hispanic tracts suggests that, as overall diversity increased, segregation endured.

Yet such persistent concentration over time does create neighborhood cultures. That is, a loose yet integrated fabric of social interactions and social institutions that enable local coordination of behaviors (e.g. differences in the products available at the corner store).

Why Does the Segregation Analysis Below Focus on People 25 and over?

Adults 25 and older are prioritized because they provide useful data indicators. History has shown that education levels among adults 25+ are strongly correlated with household income and other SES outcomes, making this group a ripe candidate for study. What's more, most adults have finished their formal education by age 25. If we were to include adults aged 18-24, our educational attainment figures would be skewed, as many individuals in this age group are still completing their education.

Ultimately, focusing on adults aged 25 and older yields more stable measurements near universities (e.g. DePaul/Chicago's Lincoln Park). Including younger groups causes these areas to present as “low education” and muddies our estimates.

Black Share x Log Household Income




Interpreting the Relationship Between Black Share x Log Household Income

A slightly negative slope is seen in this regression line, implying a negative relationship between the Black share and tract-level average household income. Still, variation remains plentiful within the plot as not all Black tracts are low-income, and not all low-income tracts are majority Black.

Essentially, this slope is imprecise and doesn't account for unobservables such as social environments, cognitive demands, socioeconomic conditions, culture, technology, and temporal context. For that reason, we cannot say much about the slope, as the differences may be due to sorting.

Nonetheless, the plot's slope and distribution still corroborate the story of Black neighborhoods in Cook County across historical time. It could be said that the Black share's lower incomes could be explained by the structural context. For example, the residential locations of Black residents are farther from areas of job growth, which may contribute to higher unemployment rates.

Hispanic Share x Log Household Income




Interpreting the Relationship Between Hispanic Share x Log Household Income

This regression line has a flatter slope than the Black share. Regarding its distribution, the lion's share of the Hispanic population is concentrated around mid-range income levels. What's more, as the share nears 0, there is a heterogeneity within the Hispanic population, with some tracts relatively well-heeled and others drawing lower incomes. This discrepancy could possibly be traced to differences in education or immigration status.

White Share x Log Household Income




Interpreting the Relationship Between White Share x Log Household Income

The regression line reports a slight positive slope, suggesting a weak relationship between white share and average household income. When examining the distribution, the values are tightly clustered on a log scale between 9 and 11. When the White share nears 1.0, points grow denser at high log income levels, advancing our original claim of a slight positive slope.

Consistent with Cook County’s historical patterns, tracts with a higher white share have a higher average household income. This spatial income divide along racial and ethnic lines could be attributed to differences in education and access to high-wage occupations, further compounded by neighborhood sorting effects over time.

Interpreting the Relationship Between Black Share x Educational Attainment

The regression line slopes downward, indicating a negative relationship between Black share and the share with 4-year degrees. Scanning the distribution, Black tracts with lower shares of residents with lower educational attainment have wide ranges.

However, this plot doesn't acknowledge how parental involvement may affect educational outcomes. Neither the direct effects of parental optimization behavior nor the indirect effects through neighborhood choice are considered in the graph above.

Hispanic Share x Educational Attainment




Interpreting the Relationship Between Hispanic Share x Educational Attainment

The fitted line slopes downward, showing that as the Hispanic share grows, educational attainment decreases. Sifting through the distribution's far right, BA rates clustered around 0-15%. Whereas along the left-hand side, there is substantial heterogeneity at low Hispanic share, reflecting predominantly white or mixed-race tracts.

Overall, this pattern is like that of the Black share. Yet the Hispanic population's low educational attainment may stem from various factors, such as language barriers and occupational sorting into jobs that don't require a bachelor’s degree.

Share of Low-Income Students Across Cook County High Schools




Interpreting Share of Low-Income Students Across Cook County High Schools

The histogram reveals that Cook County serves a largely low-income student body, yet with substantial variation across districts. In the data provided, the typical high school has a mean share of approximately 65% low-income students, a median share of roughly 76%, and a low and high of 3.3% and 100%, respectively.

Schools in the City of Chicago vs. Suburban Cook County

When comparing the two regions, the analysis shows: (1) suburban rates of economic disadvantage are half that of the city, (2) the suburban black share is also about half that of Chicago, and (3) the White share nearly triples in the suburbs. Suburban high schools also have significantly lower rates of low-income students than those in Chicago, even with reasonably similar racial/ethnic mixes.

% of low-Income Students to SAT Math Across Regions




Interpreting % of low-Income Students to SAT Math Across Regions

Low-income share is strongly associated with lower SAT math scores. This reflects concentrated disadvantage – that is, fewer resources flow to students in neighborhoods with lower household incomes, resulting in less tax revenue for school districts. Also, when we stratify schools, we see that suburban schools tend to outperform city schools in math.

However, this plot does not consider other factors that affect test scores, such as personal stressors affecting the student before the exam and/or the effects of high-arousal events (e.g. nearby violence on the day of the exam), family environment, etc.

Interpreting % of low-Income Students to SAT Reading Across Regions

Low-income share is strongly associated with lower SAT reading scores. Additionally, when we stratify by region, we again see that suburban schools tend to outperform city schools in reading.

Housing Markets and High School SAT Performance (2023)



Interpreting Housing Markets and High School SAT Performance (2023)

In the data table above, the bottom six Cook County suburban submarkets were selected and compared to the City of Chicago. When fine-tooth combing the data, lower-priced submarkets tend to have lower average SAT scores. This pattern reflects earlier correlations between neighborhood SES and educational outcomes.

Limitations of this approach include its lack of consideration for the developing adolescent. Sadly, this table merely presents a snapshot of a high schooler’s experience. Such a narrow bandwidth doesn't account for how historical changes in everyday life (e.g. shifts in the normative pattern of schooling due to the pandemic) could influence cognitively demanding tasks such as the SAT.

Relationship Between HS Drop Out Rates for Females x SAT Math



Interpreting the Relationship Between HS Dropout Rates for Females x SAT Math

The slope of the regression line is negative, suggesting that as female dropout rates increase, SAT math scores slide downward. Delving into the distribution, we see that schools with a low female dropout rate (<5%) have much higher SAT math scores. On the contrary, schools with higher dropout rates (10-20%) have significantly lower scores, often below 400.

Yet this plot doesn't capture how specific aspects of the immediate surroundings (familiarity with a college-preparatory curriculum, cognitive demands, social aspects) and sociocultural and socioeconomic contexts may be driving female dropout rates. What's more, omitted variables such as the teacher qualifications and contextual differences across time may also explain much more of this relationship than expected.
