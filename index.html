Neighborhood Racial/Ethnic Concentration Over Time



Interpreting Racial/Ethnic Concentration Over Time

Black-majority tracts rose marginally between 1970 and 2010, then settled around 21-22%. On the other hand, Hispanic-majority tracts with 80% concentration started near zero but rose to 7% by 2010. This rise reflects the increase in landing spots (e.g. Pilsen, Little Village) for new immigrants to overcome resource differences.

On the other hand, white-majority tracts dropped in numbers between 1970 and 2010. This long-running decline reflects the growing racial and ethnic diversity across Cook County. Ultimately, the constancy of unmixed Black and Hispanic tracts suggests that as overall diversity climbed, segregation endured.

This persistent concentration over time creates a neighborhood culture. That is, a loose yet integrated fabric of social interactions and institutions that enable local coordination of behaviors (e.g. differences in the products available at the corner store).

What is the Dissimilarity Index (DI)?

A common way of measuring segregation is the dissimilarity index. It calculates the share of a race/ethnicity that would need to change for races/ethnicities to be evenly distributed in a city (e.g., 0.3-0.6: moderately segregated; 0.9: highly segregated).

Cook County's Dissimilarity Index (DI)



Interpreting Cook County's Dissimilarity Index (DI)

Cook County may not feel segregated in its public spaces (e.g. downtown shopping corridors, public transit), but it certainly is in its residences. While segregation has decreased over the decades, it remains persistent in Cook County. For the races to be evenly distributed, roughly 75% of Black and Hispanic neighborhoods would need to change.

Why Do We Focus on People 25 and Older in the Analysis Below?

Adults 25 and older are prioritized because they provide more useful data indicators. History has shown that education levels among adults 25+ are strongly correlated with household income and other SES outcomes, making this group a better candidate for study.

What's more, most adults have finished their formal education by age 25. If we were to include adults aged 18-24, our educational attainment figures would be skewed, as many individuals in this age group are still completing their education.

Finally, focusing on adults aged 25 and older yields more stable measurements near universities (e.g., UIC/Little Italy). Including younger groups causes these areas to present as “low education” and muddies our estimates.

Relationship Between Black Share x Log Household Income




Interpreting Relationship Between Black Share x Log Household Income

A slightly negative slope implies a negative relationship between the Black share and tract-level log household income. Yet variation remains plentiful within the plot as not all Black tracts are low-income, and not all low-income tracts are majority Black. Basically, the slope is imprecise and doesn't account for unobservables such as cognitive demands, socioeconomic conditions, temporal context, etc. For that reason, we cannot say much about the slope, as the differences may be due to neighborhood sorting.

Nonetheless, the plot's slope and distribution still corroborate the story of Black neighborhoods in Cook County across historical time. It could be argue that the Black share's lower incomes could be explained by structural context. For example, the residential locations of Black residents are farther from areas of job growth, which may contribute to higher unemployment rates and lower log household incomes.

Relationship Between Hispanic Share x Log Household Income




Interpreting Relationship Between Hispanic Share x Log Household Income

This regression line has a slight positive slope, emblematic of a weak relationship between the two variables. Regarding its distribution, the lion's share of the Hispanic population is concentrated around mid-range income levels. What's more, as the share nears 0, there is a heterogeneity within the Hispanic population, with some tracts relatively well-heeled and others drawing lower incomes. This discrepancy could be traced to differences in education or immigration status.

Relationship Between White Share x Log Household Income




Interpreting Relationship Between White Share x Log Household Income

The regression line reports a slight positive slope, suggesting a weak relationship between white share and average household income. When examining the distribution, values are tightly clustered between the log-scale values of 9 and 11. When the White share nears 1.0, points grow denser at high log income levels, advancing our original claim of a slight positive slope.

Consistent with Cook County’s historical patterns, tracts with a higher white share have a higher average household income. This spatial income divide along racial and ethnic lines could be attributed to differences in education and access to high-wage occupations, further compounded by neighborhood sorting effects over time.

Interpreting Relationship Between Black Share x Educational Attainment

The regression line slopes downward, indicating a negative relationship between the black share and those with bachelor's degrees. Scanning the distribution, we glean that Black tracts with lower shares of residents and lower educational attainment have wider ranges.

However, this plot doesn't acknowledge how parental involvement may affect educational outcomes. Ultimately, neither the direct effects of parental optimization behavior nor the indirect effects through neighborhood choice are considered in the graph above.

Relationship Between Hispanic Share x Educational Attainment




Interpreting Relationship Between Hispanic Share x Educational Attainment

The fitted line slopes downward, showing that as the Hispanic share grows, educational attainment decreases. Sifting through the distribution's far right, we see bachelor's rates clustered around 0-15%. Whereas along the left-hand side, there is substantial heterogeneity at low Hispanic share, reflecting predominantly white or mixed-race tracts.

Overall, this pattern is like that of the Black share. Yet the Hispanic population's low rate of educational attainment may be due to various factors, such as language barriers and occupational sorting into jobs that don't require a bachelor’s degree.

Share of Low-Income Students Across Cook County High Schools




Interpreting Share of Low-Income Students Across Cook County High Schools

The histogram reveals that Cook County serves a largely low-income student body, yet with substantial variation across districts. What's more, when combing the data provided, the typical high school has a mean share of approximately 65% low-income students, a median share of roughly 76%, and a low and high of 3.3% and 100%, respectively.

Schools in the City of Chicago vs. Suburban Cook County

When comparing the two regions, the data shows that: (1) suburban rates of economic disadvantage are half that of the city, (2) the suburban black share is also about half that of Chicago, and (3) the White share nearly triples in the suburbs. Suburban high schools also have significantly lower rates of low-income students than those in Chicago, even with reasonably similar racial/ethnic mixes.

% of low-Income Students to SAT Math Across Regions




Interpreting % of low-Income Students to SAT Math Across Regions

Low-income share is strongly associated with lower SAT math scores. This reflects concentrated disadvantage – that is, fewer resources flow to students in neighborhoods with lower household incomes, resulting in less tax revenue for school districts.

Also, when we stratify schools, we see that suburban schools tend to outperform city schools in math. However, this plot does not consider other factors that affect test scores, such as personal stressors affecting the student before the exam and/or the effects of high-arousal events (e.g. nearby violence on the day of the exam), family environment, etc.

% of low-Income Students to SAT Reading Scores Across Regions




Interpreting % of low-Income Students to SAT Reading Across Regions

Low-income share is strongly associated with lower SAT reading scores. Once again, this reflects concentrated disadvantage – that is, fewer resources flow to students in neighborhoods with lower household incomes, resulting in less tax revenue for school districts. Additionally, when we stratify by region, once again suburban schools tend to outperform city schools in reading.

Interpreting Housing Markets and High School SAT Performance (2023)

In data table above the bottom six Cook Country suburban submarkets were selected and compared to the City of Chicago. When combing the data lower-priced submarkets tend to have lower average SAT scores. This pattern reflects earlier correlations between neighborhood SES and educational outcomes.

Limitations of this approach include its lack of consideration for the developing adolescent. The table above merely presents a snapshot of a high schooler’s experience. Such a narrow bandwidth doesn't account for how historical changes in everyday life could influence SAT scores (e.g. shifts in the normative pattern of schooling due to the pandemic).

Relationship Between HS Drop Out Rates for Females x SAT Math Scores



Interpreting HS Drop Out Rates for Females x SAT Math Scores

The slope of the regression line is negative, suggesting that as female dropout rates increase SAT math scores slide downward. Delving into the distribution, we see that schools with a low female dropout rate (<5%) have much higher SAT math scores. On the contrary, schools with higher dropout rates (10-20%) have significantly lower scores, often below 400.

Yet this plot doesn't capture how specific aspects of the immediate surroundings (familiarity with a college preparatory curriculum, cognitive demands, social aspects) and socio-cultural and socioeconomic contexts may be driving female dropout rates. What's more, omitted variables such as the teacher qualifications and contextual differences across time may also explain much more of this relationship than expected.
